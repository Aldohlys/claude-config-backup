Exiting a losing trade is mechanically easier than exiting a winner. The loser's exit is forced (stop hit, or pain crystallized); the winner's exit requires actively choosing to end profit that could continue.

**The asymmetry (user's framing, 2026-05-18):** "It is hard to get out of a winning trade — much easier to get out of a losing trade."

**Cognitive trap:** With a winner at target, the brain reframes the situation:
- "But what if it goes higher?"
- "The news supports continuation"
- "I'll just trail tighter instead of exit"
- "Let me hold one more bar"

Every framing pushes toward "hold." With a loser, the brain finalizes faster ("cut it, move on, free the capital").

**Why this matters:** This asymmetry systematically reduces realized profits relative to plan. Even when the trade hits its structural target cleanly, the exit decision feels difficult — there's always a narrative (macro, technical, momentum) offering reasons to extend.

**Concrete example (MCL Jul26, 2026-05-18):** Hit \$102 structural target after 7 days. The exit was correct by every framework metric (no asymmetry left, prior resistance, expected profit range achieved). Still felt hard — macro bullish narrative was actively offering reasons to extend.

**How to apply:**
1. **Pre-commit the exit.** Limit orders at the structural target, transmitted BEFORE the price approaches it. The order does the exit; you don't have to choose in the moment.
2. **Write the exit criteria down at entry.** "I exit at \$X because \$X is prior resistance and asymmetry runs out there." When tempted to override, re-read the entry note.
3. **Treat 'almost target' as 'target hit.'** If you got 90%+ of the planned profit, the trade succeeded — don't re-litigate to capture the last 10%.
4. **Runner SL discipline:** if keeping a runner past partial scale-out, the SL must sit meaningfully BELOW the scale-out level (\$1-2, not \$0.10). Same-level SL = delayed exit at scale-out price, not a real runner.
